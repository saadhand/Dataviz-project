Earnings Interpretation

We notice that the highest wages are located in central and southwest London. In first place, we find Kensington and Chelsea followed by Wandsworth. This can give us an idea of the real estate prices and the social class of the residents. We can assume that the people who earn the most will have the best investment decisions so investing in these neighbourhoods would be a good choice, is this the case?

Crimes Interpretation

Compared to what we saw in the first graph, we notice that the highest number of crimes is found in the poorest neighbourhoods. It may affect that these are dangerous places where people do not prefer to invest.

HPI Interpretation

This graph shows the evolution of the House Price Index by European countries from 2011 to 2021. We see that in 2012, all the HPIs were quite at the same level and it started diverging by years. Comparing the HPI of England to the other countries, we can say it stayed quite at the same level (between 85 and 120) by a small increase in this last 10 years which means that the UK sector is stable comparing to the other countries with a bullish trend.

Economical Interpretation

This graph shows the variation of the HPI in England and the UK interest and inflation rate. The HPI kept going up from 2012 to 2021 whereas the inflation and interest rates kept fluctuating in this period which means that the England HPI is not impacted by the rates’ movements.

Unemployment Interpretation

Unemployment in the UK region is quite high at the beginning of the period and even higher in the City of London. Indeed, in 2011, unemployment was around 10% for the City of London, while the rest of the UK averaged around 8%. Over time, although unemployment levels are falling significantly, London remains a city with higher than average unemployment.

Price to Earning Regional ratio Interpretation

Three groups of regions can be clearly distinguished which follow more or less the same trend. In the first position, we notice that London has a very high Price to Earnings. The increase of this ratio is also very important in this region. This is not necessarily a good investment signal. As far as the others are concerned, we note a slight increase in the ratio between 2008 and 2020. Only the North East region seems to have had an increase in income compared to the price of real estate.

Price to Earning Borough ratio Interpretation

Within the London area itself, it is quickly apparent that values were already at 10 in 2008. This means that in order to buy a property, one had to invest 10 times the annual income received, which is very important. We can also see immediately that even in 2008, the central London areas were reaching very high levels. Finally, over time, between 2008 and 2020, all areas have seen a significant increase in property prices. Indeed, the ratios doubled on average to reach levels of 30 for Kensington and Chelsea.

Price to Rent ratio Interpretation

Over time, the price to rent ratio has increased too much. It was more interesting before buying, but our price to rent ratio has been too high since 2012. Now, with the latest data we have at the end of 2019, the Price to Rent Ratio of the different neighbourhoods is above 21, which recommends us to rent, much better than to buy. We can assume that the market is overvalued.

Property Interpretation

The upward trend is very clear in this graph. The IPRHP has increased across England. We can also see that although London is not the region with the highest index, it is still the region that has had the largest increase in its Index of Private Rental Housing Price.

Snapshot Interpretation

In this table, we have tried to compare the UK with 15 other European based countries. We note that for all the indicators we are trying to present, UK is in the average range. So there is not much of a stake in investing there.

Forecast Interpretation

This chart shows the Annual Cumulative Price Change Rate Forecast of rentals in London from 2020 to 2024. For an investment in 2020, there will be a 15.5% increase in rental prices. Conclusion, we are in front of a great opportunity, but it is now!
